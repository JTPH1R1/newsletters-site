## 🌍 Dangote's $40 Billion Refinery Is Going Public — Africa's Biggest IPO Ever Is Coming This June

### AFRICA | FINANCE

> **Source:** [African Business](https://african.business/2026/04/finance-services/dangote-prepares-40-billion-refinery-ipo-across-multiple-exchanges) / [Further Africa](https://furtherafrica.com/2026/05/01/dangote-refinery-ipo-plans-40bn-listing-across-africa/) · 1–5 May 2026

### What Happened

Aliko Dangote has confirmed plans to list approximately 10% of the Dangote Petroleum Refinery in a $40 billion IPO across multiple African stock exchanges, targeting a formal listing on the Nigerian Exchange Group between June and July 2026. The NGX has already convened chief executives from five major African exchanges — the Johannesburg Stock Exchange, the Ghana Stock Exchange, the Nairobi Securities Exchange, the Ethiopian Securities Exchange, and the Bourse Régionale des Valeurs Mobilières — to advance a simultaneous cross-border listing. A prospectus has been submitted to Nigeria's SEC, a national retail roadshow is underway, and an electronic IPO subscription platform launches this month.

### What It Means

This is not a routine listing. The Dangote Refinery is Africa's largest oil refinery, processing 650,000 barrels per day — a facility that directly affects fuel prices, fertiliser costs, and foreign exchange dynamics across the entire continent. At a $40 billion valuation, this would be the single largest equity offering in African capital market history. The decision to list across five exchanges simultaneously is a deliberate signal: Dangote and the NGX are testing whether African markets can function as an integrated, continent-wide capital system rather than isolated national pools. If it works, it creates a template for every major African infrastructure asset that follows. If retail investors across Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, Senegal, and Ethiopia can all buy shares in the same offering on the same day, that changes what African capital markets mean — not just for institutions, but for professionals and savers who have never had access to this class of asset. The strategic goal behind the IPO is also operational: proceeds will fund a doubling of refinery capacity and a quadrupling of fertiliser production — two inputs that directly affect Malawi's fuel import bill and agricultural input costs.

> **What To Watch:** Track the prospectus approval timeline from Nigeria's SEC and watch whether the May retail roadshow generates institutional demand from East and Southern African fund managers. If the cross-border subscription platform works at scale, this sets a precedent for how African markets integrate going forward.

## 📍 Malawi Traders Shut Down Over E-Invoicing — A Tax Reform Crisis Hits the Streets

### MALAWI | ECONOMY

> **Source:** [Mail & Guardian](https://mg.co.za/africa/2026-05-04-malawi-trader-shutdown-exposes-deeper-tension-over-tax-reform-and-economic-crisis/) · 4 May 2026

### What Happened

Traders across Lilongwe and Blantyre closed their shops this week in coordinated protest against the government's rollout of an electronic invoicing system. Wholesale markets and retail outlets in major trading districts shut their doors, disrupting supply chains that feed small retailers and households across the country. The shutdown exposed deep tension between the Malawi Revenue Authority's push to formalise tax collection and a trading community already stretched thin by inflation, high import costs, and weak consumer demand.

### What It Means

The e-invoicing system is not the real issue — it is the trigger. Traders are not protesting technology. They are protesting the compounding pressure of operating in an economy where inflation is still above 24%, the Kwacha continues to weaken, fuel costs remain elevated, and now a new compliance burden arrives with no transition support. For the government, the dilemma is real: Malawi needs to broaden its tax base and reduce dependence on aid — but pushing compliance too fast on businesses already at the margin risks destroying the informal economy that employs the majority of Malawians. The IMF has been explicit this week that aid cuts of 16 to 28% across Sub-Saharan Africa are exposing countries like Malawi that have not yet built sustainable domestic revenue systems. The e-invoicing rollout is the right long-term direction. The timing and execution are the problem.

> **What To Watch:** Watch whether the MRA offers a grace period or transition support to traders. If the shutdown spreads or extends into next week, it signals that the government has a communication and implementation failure on its hands — not just a compliance problem.

## 📍 RBM Holds Rate at 24% — Inflation Eases But the Pressure Is Not Over

### MALAWI | ECONOMY

> **Source:** [Nyasa Times](https://www.nyasatimes.com/rbm-keeps-interest-rate-at-24-while-tightening-bank-lending-to-control-inflation-and-stabilise-the-economy/) · May 2026

### What Happened

The Reserve Bank of Malawi held its benchmark interest rate at 24% at its latest monetary policy committee meeting. The decision followed a modest improvement in inflation — dropping from 27.7% in late 2025 to 24.3% in Q1 2026 — driven primarily by improved food supply. The RBM simultaneously tightened bank lending conditions to further suppress demand-side inflation. GDP growth is projected to improve to 3.8% in 2026, up from 2.7% in 2025, led by agriculture, mining, and manufacturing.

### What It Means

The rate hold is a carefully balanced signal. Cutting rates now would risk reigniting inflation — particularly non-food inflation, which remains driven by fuel costs and expensive imports that the RBM cannot control. Holding at 24% means borrowing remains prohibitively expensive for most Malawian businesses and households, which suppresses the very investment and consumption that would drive the 3.8% growth projection. The improvement in food inflation is real but fragile — it is weather-dependent, and any supply shock reverses it. The IMF's warning this week that aid cuts are hitting Malawi hard adds another pressure point: if budget support gaps emerge, the government may need domestic borrowing, which competes with private sector credit and keeps rates elevated. The path forward — lower inflation, lower rates, higher growth — is visible. Getting there requires sustained fiscal discipline, continued agricultural improvement, and external stability that Malawi cannot fully control.

> **What To Watch:** Watch the May inflation release and track whether non-food inflation begins to respond to the lending tightening. If food prices spike again before the June MPC meeting, a rate cut becomes even less likely before Q3.
